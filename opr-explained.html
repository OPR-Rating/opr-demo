OPR - OPEN PADEL RATING

OPR is a rating system built from the ground up for doubles padel — Men's, Ladies, Mixed and All Sorts pairings — for the real world of club play, where partners change and combinations vary. It gives every player a fair, transparent rating that reflects how they actually play, and gives the club the tools to run ladders and tournaments on its own, any day of the week.

The idea

What OPR is

A complete rating and competition platform that a padel club owns and runs itself — no monthly per-player fees, no outside gatekeeper.

Most rating systems borrow a singles model from tennis or squash and bolt doubles on afterwards. OPR was built the other way round — designed specifically for doubles and mixed-pairs padel, where partners change, gender combinations vary, and match volume is limited. It handles Men's, Ladies, Mixed and All Sorts pairings natively, with the stronger partner correctly weighted in mixed and uneven pairs.

And it's owned by the club. Most systems keep your members' data on a platform you don't control, with numbers from a black box. OPR flips that: each club gets its own private space — its own members, ladders and tournaments — with a rating engine explained openly so players trust it.

That's the real difference. On a global app you're just one more anonymous player in a giant pool. With OPR you belong to your club — you and your regular partner have a standing, a story and a place in your community, not a faceless worldwide ladder. And because padel is a game of doubles, OPR is built around exactly that: you and your partner, rising and falling together, known by name at the club you actually play at. For the club itself, OPR becomes part of what membership means — a reason to join, a reason to stay, and something that's genuinely the club's own.

How the rating works

Player ratings

Your rating is a single number that reflects your real playing strength — and it moves every time you play.

The core principle is simple: OPR scores how you played, not just the final result. A match where you take a set and push a strong pair close says far more about your level than a lucky win — and OPR captures that.

Every match result blends two things: the sets and the games.
Sets carry the most weight (winning the match matters most), while the games within those sets fine-tune the picture — so a 6–0, 6–1 win and a 7–6, 7–6 win are correctly treated as very different performances.

From there, four ideas shape the number:

  • Performance, not just the win. How dominant or close the match was feeds directly into the rating change.
  • Who you played matters. Results are weighed against the strength of your opponents — beating a stronger pair moves you more.
  • It updates match by match. Ratings are rebuilt in order from your full history, so the number always reflects your current level, not a stale snapshot.
  • One rating, all formats. Singles, doubles, mixed — they feed a single universal rating, with sensible weighting so a strong player carrying a weaker partner is judged fairly.

Doubles — how a pair's strength is set

For same-gender pairs, the team strength is the blend of both players. For mixed or mixed-ability pairs, the stronger player carries a little more of the weight — because that's how the game actually plays out on court. The result is a pairing rating that feels right to the people who played.

Levels — the friendly version of the number

Alongside the precise rating, OPR shows a simpler level — an at-a-glance band that members instantly understand and can use to find well-matched games. The detailed rating drives the system; the level makes it human.

Recent shape

Form

Your rating is your overall level. Your form is whether you're hot or cold right now.

Form looks at your recent results and shows whether you've been trending up or down lately — the padel equivalent of "in good touch." It's separate from your rating on purpose: one bad week shouldn't crater your standing, but it should be visible.

  • Recent-weighted. Your latest matches count for more than ones from months ago.
  • Needs a real sample. Form only shows once you've played enough to be meaningful — it won't over-react to a single match.
  • A coaching cue. A dipping form line is a nudge; a rising one is a reward. It keeps players engaged between events.
Ongoing competition

The Club Ladder

A living leaderboard of pairs, where you climb by challenging the teams above you.

The ladder is the club's heartbeat between tournaments. Pairs sign up, the ladder sets an opening order by rating, and from there it's all earned on court.

1

Teams join. Pairs enter the ladder for their category — Men's, Ladies, Mixed or All Sorts.

2

The ladder activates. Opening positions are set by combined rating — strongest at the top — then it's frozen and the climbing begins.

3

Challenge upward. Beat a team above you and you take their spot; they move down. Every result reshapes the ladder.

4

The club stays in control. Admins can reshuffle to rating order, or wipe and start a fresh season whenever they like.

Events, run by you

Tournaments

Run a complete, fair tournament — group stages, finals and a champion — in a single afternoon, with no outside help.

This is what lets a club host a Saturday social, an office function, or a proper club championship entirely on its own. OPR handles the hard part: pairing teams fairly so nobody gets a string of mismatches, and so the best teams genuinely rise to the top.

Banded, fair pairings

Teams are grouped into strength bands so games stay competitive — no top seed steamrolling a beginner in round one. Fairness is the rule the draw bends to, never the other way around.

Swiss-style rounds that scale

After a few fixed opening rounds, OPR generates further rounds based on the standings — winners meet winners — with no team ever playing the same opponent twice. The number of rounds adapts to the size of the field.

Divisions for bigger fields

When a category is large enough, it splits into Division A and Division B by strength — so mid-field teams have their own real competition and their own champion, not just the top seeds sweeping everything.

Semi-finals & a final

When the rounds are done, the top four in each division play off — 1 v 4, 2 v 3 — and the winners contest the final. The champion is crowned automatically. A real finish to a real event.

Results entered during the event flow straight into players' ratings and stats — so the tournament isn't a side-show, it's part of everyone's ongoing OPR story.

An "All Sorts" division for anything goes

Alongside Men's, Ladies and Mixed, OPR has an All Sorts category — for bespoke or unconventional pairings that don't fit the usual boxes. Perfect for socials, mixed-ability fun days, office functions or any "just put a team together" event. The same fair engine rates it; you just don't have to force everyone into traditional divisions.

Behind the scenes

The Admin Control Room

Everything the club needs to run itself sits in one secure place.

The club's organiser logs into the Admin Control Room to manage the whole operation — no technical know-how needed, and nothing exposed to ordinary members.

  • Manage members — add new players, edit details, keep the roster current.
  • Create & run tournaments — set up events, choose divisions and caps, generate the draws.
  • Edit or correct results — fix a wrongly-entered score or remove a match if needed; ratings recalculate automatically.
  • Run the ladders — activate, reshuffle, or reset a season.
Every score entry is protected by a PIN.
Results can only be submitted with a valid PIN — a player's own, the club admin's, or the master code — so the data stays trustworthy and nobody can quietly tamper with the standings. Fair ratings depend on clean inputs, and the PIN is what guards them.

The story

About OPR

OPR was built by a padel player, for padel clubs — out of a simple frustration.

Existing systems were either locked behind big platforms, expensive per player, or impossible to understand. None of them let a club simply own its own ratings and run its own events. So OPR was built from the ground up to do exactly that: a fair engine, a clean app, and a model where the club is in charge — not a faceless platform.

It's been proven on real club data — hundreds of players and hundreds of matches — and it's ready for clubs anywhere to make their own.
